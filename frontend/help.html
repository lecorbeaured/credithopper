Getting Started

CreditHopper is a self-service credit repair platform that helps you dispute inaccurate, incomplete, or unverifiable items on your credit report. Here's how it works:

  1. Upload your credit report from Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion
  2. Our Credit Repair Engine analyzes it and identifies potential issues
  3. Select the items you want to dispute from the identified issues
  4. We generate professional dispute letters customized to your situation
  5. Print and mail the letters via certified mail to the credit bureaus
  6. Track your progress in your dashboard

The entire process leverages your legal rights under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA), which requires credit bureaus to investigate and verify disputed information within 30 days.

You're entitled to one free credit report from each bureau every year. Here's how to get them:

  1. Visit AnnualCreditReport.com (the only authorized source)
  2. Select all three bureaus: Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion
  3. Complete the identity verification process
  4. Download your reports as PDF files
  5. Upload them to CreditHopper's Credit Repair Engine

Pro Tip: Get reports from all three bureaus. Creditors don't always report to all three, so discrepancies may only appear on one report.

The timeline varies depending on your situation, but here's a general overview:

  • Letter delivery: 3-7 business days via certified mail
  • Bureau investigation: 30-45 days (legally required)
  • Results notification: Within 5 days of completing investigation
  • Overall process: 2-6 months for significant improvements

Most users see their first items removed within 30-45 days of sending their first dispute letters. For best results, we recommend disputing items in rounds and allowing each round to complete before starting the next.

Credit Basics

Your FICO credit score is calculated based on five main factors:

Payment History (35%) On-time payments vs. late payments, collections, and defaults
Credit Utilization (30%) How much of your available credit you're using (keep under 30%)
Length of Credit History (15%) How long you've had credit accounts open
Credit Mix (10%) Variety of credit types (cards, loans, mortgage)
New Credit (10%) Recent credit inquiries and new accounts

Hard Inquiries occur when you apply for credit (credit card, loan, mortgage). They:

  • Can lower your score by 5-10 points
  • Stay on your report for 2 years
  • Only affect your score for 12 months
  • Can be disputed if unauthorized

Soft Inquiries occur when you check your own credit or a company pre-approves you. They:

  • Do NOT affect your credit score
  • Are only visible to you
  • Include employer background checks

Different types of negative items have different reporting periods:

  • Late payments: 7 years from the date of the missed payment
  • Collections: 7 years from the original delinquency date
  • Charge-offs: 7 years from the date of the charge-off
  • Chapter 7 bankruptcy: 10 years from filing date
  • Chapter 13 bankruptcy: 7 years from filing date
  • Hard inquiries: 2 years (only affects score for 12 months)
  • Tax liens: 7 years (if paid) or indefinitely (if unpaid)*

*Note: As of 2018, tax liens are no longer included on credit reports from the major bureaus.

The FCRA is a federal law that gives you important rights regarding your credit report:

  • Access: You have the right to see what's in your credit file
  • Dispute: You can dispute inaccurate or incomplete information
  • Investigation: Bureaus must investigate disputes within 30 days
  • Verification: Information must be verified or removed
  • Notification: You must be notified of negative items
  • Consent: Your credit can only be accessed with permissible purpose

CreditHopper's dispute letters are designed to exercise your rights under the FCRA effectively.

Disputes & Letters

You can dispute any information you believe to be:

  • Inaccurate: Wrong balances, dates, account status, or personal info
  • Incomplete: Missing information that would improve your standing
  • Unverifiable: Items the creditor cannot prove with documentation
  • Outdated: Information that should have been removed
  • Fraudulent: Accounts opened without your authorization

Important: Only dispute items you genuinely believe to be inaccurate. Disputing accurate information is not only ineffective but may constitute fraud.

Follow these steps for the best results:

  1. Print the letter on plain white paper
  2. Type your full name at the bottom (do not hand-sign)
  3. Make a copy for your records
  4. Include supporting documents (copy of ID, proof of address)
  5. Send via Certified Mail with Return Receipt Requested
  6. Keep the tracking number and green receipt card

Important: Do NOT hand-sign your dispute letters. A typed name is legally sufficient and prevents your signature from being used to verify accounts.

Here's the typical timeline after sending a dispute:

1
Receipt (3-7 days)

Bureau receives your letter via certified mail

2
Investigation begins (within 5 days)

Bureau contacts the creditor/data furnisher

3
Verification period (30-45 days)

Creditor must verify the information or it gets removed

4
Results notification (within 5 days of completion)

You receive results by mail with updated report

Possible outcomes: Item removed, item updated/corrected, or item verified as accurate.

Don't give up! You have several options:

  1. Request Method of Verification: Ask the bureau how the item was verified
  2. Dispute directly with the creditor: Sometimes more effective than bureau disputes
  3. Add a consumer statement: 100-word explanation added to your report
  4. Submit additional evidence: New documentation may change the outcome
  5. File a CFPB complaint: If you believe your rights were violated
  6. Consult an attorney: For persistent violations of your rights

Our Letter Library includes follow-up templates for each of these scenarios.

We strongly recommend disputing by mail for several reasons:

  • Paper trail: Certified mail provides legal proof of delivery
  • More detail: Letters allow for thorough explanations
  • Supporting documents: You can include evidence with your letter
  • Better protection: Online disputes often include arbitration clauses
  • Higher success rate: Many users report better results with mail

Online disputes are faster but offer less control and documentation. Use mail for important disputes.
