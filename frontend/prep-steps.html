Critical Steps Before You Dispute

Complete these steps BEFORE uploading your credit report or sending any letters. Skipping these can sabotage your entire credit repair journey.

Do NOT Skip This Section

The credit bureaus and collectors have systems designed to make disputing harder. They track your behavior, flag "problem" consumers, and use your own actions against you. These steps protect you and dramatically increase your success rate.

1
Freeze Your Secondary Bureau Reports Critical
Prevent creditors from pulling alternative reports to verify disputed info

Why This Matters

When you dispute items with the Big 3 bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion), they often verify information by checking secondary bureaus - smaller credit reporting agencies you've probably never heard of. If these secondary bureaus have records of the same accounts, the Big 3 will use that to "verify" the disputed item and deny your dispute.

By freezing your secondary bureau reports FIRST, you cut off this verification path. When the creditor or collection agency can't verify through these channels, they're more likely to fail verification - and items that can't be verified must be deleted.

Freeze These 4 Secondary Bureaus

LexisNexis

Major data broker used by creditors and collectors

Freeze Now
ChexSystems

Tracks banking history, used for account verification

Freeze Now
Innovis

4th largest bureau, often used for soft verification

Freeze Now
NCTUE (National Consumer Telecom)

Tracks utility and telecom payment history

Freeze Now

Do this BEFORE disputing anything. If you've already sent disputes, freeze these immediately - it will still help with future disputes and re-investigations.

Remember to Unfreeze When Needed

These freezes will block legitimate credit checks too. You'll need to temporarily unfreeze these bureaus when you:

  • Apply for a mortgage or auto loan - Lenders check LexisNexis and Innovis
  • Open a new credit card - Some issuers use secondary bureaus
  • Open a bank account - Banks use ChexSystems to verify banking history
  • Sign up for utilities - Electric, gas, and water companies check NCTUE
  • Get a cell phone plan - Carriers check NCTUE for payment history
  • Rent an apartment - Landlords may use LexisNexis for tenant screening

Tip: Unfreeze 2-3 days before applying, then re-freeze immediately after. Each bureau lets you set a temporary lift for a specific date range.

2
Do NOT Create Online Accounts with Bureaus Danger
Avoid Equifax, Experian, TransUnion, and Credit Karma accounts

Why This Matters

When you create an account with a credit bureau or monitoring service, you agree to their Terms of Service. Buried in that fine print are clauses that can hurt you:

  • Arbitration clauses - You waive your right to sue them in court
  • E-OSCAR verification - They can use their electronic system to "verify" items in seconds (no real investigation)
  • Data sharing - They share your activity with creditors and collectors
  • Dispute limitations - Some ToS limit how you can dispute or what you can dispute

By staying off their platforms, you preserve all your legal rights under the FCRA. You force them to do REAL investigations via mail, which takes time and effort - and often results in deletions because they can't be bothered.

If you already have accounts: Don't panic. But stop using them for disputes. Always dispute by certified mail going forward. Consider closing the accounts if possible.

3
NEVER Dispute Online Danger
Online disputes are designed to fail - always use certified mail

Why This Matters

The bureaus WANT you to dispute online. Why? Because their online system (E-OSCAR) is automated and designed to reject disputes quickly:

  • No paper trail - You can't prove what you submitted or when
  • Automated "verification" - E-OSCAR sends electronic pings to creditors who auto-verify in seconds
  • Limited dispute reasons - You're forced to pick from a dropdown menu, limiting your legal arguments
  • No documentation - You can't attach supporting evidence effectively
  • Harder to sue - Without certified mail receipts, you have weak evidence in court

Certified mail disputes force a real investigation. Someone has to actually open your letter, read it, and manually process it. This takes time and resources - and when creditors can't or won't provide verification documents, the item gets deleted.

Always use certified mail with return receipt requested. This creates legal proof of delivery and starts the 30-day investigation clock. Keep copies of EVERYTHING you send.

4
NEVER Agree to Anything Over the Phone Danger
Collectors record calls and will use your words against you

Why This Matters

When collectors or creditors call you, they're fishing for information and admissions. Everything you say can and will be used against you:

  • "Is this your debt?" - Saying yes restarts the statute of limitations in some states
  • "Can you make a small payment?" - Any payment, even $1, can restart the clock and validate the debt
  • "We'll settle for 50%" - Verbal agreements aren't enforceable and they'll still report the full amount
  • Recorded calls - They record everything and use it as evidence of debt acknowledgment

Everything in writing. Always. If someone calls about a debt, tell them to send all communication in writing and hang up. Never confirm any information, never acknowledge any debt, never agree to anything verbally.

What to Say When They Call

"I do not discuss financial matters over the phone. Please send all communications in writing to my address. Do not call me again. Goodbye."

Then hang up. Don't engage further. If they continue calling, that's a FDCPA violation you can sue for.

5
Do NOT Sign Your Dispute Letters Important
Your signature can be used to verify accounts and validate debts

Why This Matters

Your signature is a valuable piece of verification data. When you sign dispute letters:

  • Signature comparison - Creditors can compare it to account applications to "verify" the account is yours
  • Acknowledges receipt - It confirms you received and read their correspondence
  • Identity confirmation - It proves you are who you say you are, validating their records

Just type your name. A typed name is sufficient for dispute letters. The law doesn't require a handwritten signature for FCRA disputes. Including your ID copy is enough for identity verification.

Note: Our letter templates don't include signature lines. Just type your full name at the bottom. Include a copy of your ID and a utility bill for identity verification.

6
Check the FTC Banned Collectors List Before Disputing
If your collector is banned, you may have additional legal options

Why This Matters

The FTC maintains a list of debt collectors who have been permanently banned by federal court orders from participating in the debt collection business. These companies and individuals engaged in practices so egregious - fake debts, threats, harassment, fraud - that courts banned them entirely.

If a collector on your credit report is on this list:

  • The debt may be invalid - Many banned collectors dealt in "phantom debts" that consumers never actually owed
  • Stronger dispute grounds - You can cite their banned status in your dispute letters
  • Potential legal action - If they're still operating, they're violating a federal court order
  • FTC refunds - You may be entitled to refunds if you paid a banned collector
